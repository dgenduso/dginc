# Article Ideas

## Core Concepts

### Start with the Trash
The system works on the unglamorous problems first — trash, dead planters, scooters on sidewalks. Nobody is politically divided on these. Everyone agrees they're problems. The question is just why they keep happening and what actually needs to change. Starting here builds the process, demonstrates it works, and rebuilds resident trust in small, visible ways — before applying it to harder things.

### The Problem with Proposed Solutions
Gun control. Charter reform. Rent control. The proposed solution becomes the battleground while the original problem stays unsolved and unexamined. This is a universal pattern: problem stays high-level, a solution becomes the stand-in, debate replaces understanding, nothing changes. The fix isn't a better solution — it's a better process for understanding the problem first.

### Why the Same Problems Keep Coming Back
Every election cycle, every boom and bust — the same problems reappear. Skyrocketing rents, displacement, unaffordable cities. A partial measure passes or doesn't. The cycle continues. The reason: nothing at the root ever changed. A structured problem-solving process would break the cycle. Without it, cities just rebuild the same way they did before and the same thing happens again.

### What If the Improvements Kept Compounding After You're Gone?
Every administration comes in with a list of things they're going to do, declares some wins, and leaves. The next one starts over. No institutional learning carries through. What if it did? The case for building a system that accumulates understanding across administrations and keeps improving — outlasting any single politician or term.

### Responsive Government Is a Talking Point
Politicians promise "a government that works" and "responsive government" — but never define what that means or what's actually preventing it. They identify symptoms (slow procurement, not enough executive authority) and propose structural fixes that don't touch the root cause. The problem-solving process itself is broken. You can't fix government with a broken problem-solving process.

## Learning Organization

### From Workers to Learners: The Identity Shift That Changes Everything
Most organizational transformations focus on processes and tools. The ones that stick start somewhere deeper — with how the organization understands itself. The shift from "we are workers" to "we are learners" changes how people approach their daily work, how they interact with customers, and what they consider success.

### What AI Makes Possible: Learning from People at Scale
For decades, organizations served large populations based on limited signals. A mayor can't have 50,000 conversations. But a learning organization with the right systems can capture, synthesize, and act on 50,000 voices. AI has changed the equation — and the organizations that recognize this will operate differently.

### Working Backwards, Learning First
Steve Jobs was relentless about working backwards from the experience. But he trusted his own intuition about what that experience should be. The evolution: use AI to deeply understand what people actually need, define the experience from that, then work backwards through the organization to deliver it. Jobs' discipline, grounded in real learning rather than intuition.

### The Infinity Loop: How Learning and Improvement Connect
Most organizations have two disconnected loops — external interactions (broadcasting, marketing) and internal work (decisions made in conference rooms). Nothing crosses over. The continuous learning model reconnects them: external engagement flows inward as understanding, internal work flows back out as better service. The crossover point is where most organizations are completely broken — and where the biggest opportunity lives.

## City Government

### The City Government Opportunity
City governments touch millions of residents every year. Most of those interactions generate no learning. Engagement is sparse, feedback slow, decisions largely top-down. New forms of digital engagement and AI are changing that. The opportunity to understand residents more deeply than ever before — and continuously improve how cities serve them — has never been greater.

### The Lost Opportunity in Every Resident Event
The mayor stood 10 feet from 200 residents who had something to say. He spent the evening talking to cameras. Every event, every canvass, every service interaction is a learning moment that goes uncaptured. What a learning-first government would do differently — and what it would produce over time.

### The Affordability Crisis Nobody Is Actually Solving
"Affordability crisis" is a talking point, not a defined problem. Nobody has broken it down into its component parts, identified root causes, or defined what needs to change in the resident experience. Until that happens, proposed solutions will keep missing. Here's what actually understanding the problem would require.

### The Real Cost of Living Problem: Who Has Equity When the City Grows?
The cost of living framing is wrong. The question isn't "how do we lower costs" — it's "why do so few residents have equity in the city's growth?" Every boom cycle, people with existing equity make money and buy more. Most residents have no equity and get left behind, widening the wealth gap further. It's not about taxing the wealthy. It's about how any resident can build ownership in the things that grow when the city grows — so more people participate in the upside instead of just absorbing the cost of it.

## Process & Methods

### Innovation Is Made of Nested Learning Cycles
The 5D framework looks linear from the outside — five phases, one after another. Inside, it's something more interesting: learning cycles nested within learning cycles. Discover is a learning cycle. Diagnose is a learning cycle informed by Discover. Each phase deepens the learning of the last. AI's role changes at each level — from capturing and synthesizing customer signal, to helping diagnose causes, to evaluating whether the delivered solution actually changed the experience. Understanding this nesting is the key to understanding why the sequence can't be shortcut, and why the process compounds over time.

### What It Means to Serve
Serving starts with understanding. One interaction can lead to immediate action, future evolution, or both. Learning from people is part of serving them — not a separate activity that happens before the "real work" begins.

### Breaking Problems Down: Why It Matters Before You Propose Solutions
The step that almost never happens: decomposing a high-level problem into its distinct component parts, each with its own causes, requirements, and potential solutions. Without this, solutions address symptoms. With it, solutions actually change something in the resident experience.

### Trust Is Built Through Understanding
Genuine understanding builds trust. Work that reflects understanding deepens trust further. The connection between learning from residents and rebuilding public confidence in government — and why superficial engagement does more damage than none at all.
